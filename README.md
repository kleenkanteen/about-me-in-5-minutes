## My motto: Be bold, be curious. 

Don't be afraid to voice your disagreement, concerns, and in asking questions. Fortune favors the bold.

Both within university classes and in my first 1 year internship as a full stack developer (in currently), I'm vocal and constantly asking questions.

--

Everyone has superpowers, things they are especially good at. I have 4: being bold, being resourceful, being curious, and being a debater. Let me explain:

## Being bold
You'll see me raising my hand asking questions in class, where there's 100-200 people. I don't mind, me wanting to understand it is more important to me than looking dumb by asking questions. Or just being nervous to talk in front of the whole class. My desire to learn makes me bold. Same thing happens with asking questions in a class of 20-30 people taught by a upper year student who is hired to help the professor by the former holding their own classes and to show how to do practice problems. Most people I've observed never raise their hand to ask even in these small 20-30 person class. 100-200 seems intimading, ok, but 20-30 is less so. The hardest class I took in first year was math proofs, and in the 20-30 persons class, only me a and 2 others in a class of 25 ever spoke to ask a question. I haven't heard the voice of most of the class. I have the feeling many people were just as boggled as me but were too nervous to ask a question publicly.

## Being resourceful
I can navigate the internet well, a galaxy of information and sources. I use google searche queries; exact match, date range, AND and OR operators, exclusion operator. I say the search input in different ways if the initial results don't satisfy me. I know how to identify the good sources from the bad, both for learning sources and news sources. I know the right questions to ask and chart my own path to learn or achieve something. Having a intense curiosity and a critical mindset allowed me to hone the skill of guiding myself. Especially helpful for teaching myself something and which course/youtube channel to learn from. If I want to learn react on Udemy, there's like 20 different courses teaching it. I don't sort through them all and compare, but I look at some indicators, such as how active the instructor is in Q&A. Someone online told me instructor activity means how much they care about their students and therefore the quality of their teaching. I have used this kind thinking to choose a good quality course/youtube video/instructor and have done so many times in high school and uni to teach myself something.

## Being curious
I have a insatiable curioisity and feed the beast by asking questions. 

One example of being curious: In 4th grade I took apart a battery powered dancing flower from the dollar store that also had a small solar panel on it. I took it apart to observe how it worked. What especially interested me was the small electic motor that made the flower move left and right. But I observed something I could not figure out the answer to until years later in grade 11, when I took a physics class that talked about electric motors, and I wasn't satisfied with the teacher's explanation. As it didn't answer the question I had about motors when I observed the dancing flower's motor in 4th grade.

When I took apart the dancing flower's motor, I connected it to the battery and saw how worked. But it spinned for just one 1 rotation and then stayed in place. Readjusted it, same results. Tried to make further adjustments, still couldn't get it to spin continuously, so I left it and didn't think about it again. Years later, in grade 11 physics, we were taught how a electric motor works. I remember it I still didn't understand why when I played with one years ago, it only spun once and not continuously. I went home that day and on youtube watched a couple videos explaining how a eletric motor works, and as I watched closely, I still didn't understand why the motor kept on spinning instead of just I like I observed. What I was learning and what I observed years ago was not matching up. 

Finally I found a video of someone who explained it properly and it made perfect sense. It was a subtle but important detail. You can ask me this question and even now I'll be able to answer: What allows a electric motor to spin continuosly instead of just at most, 1 rotation?  I don't think most of my classmates there would be able to answer this question with what they learnt from the lesson taught to us. I wanted to answer this question I had thought about in 4th grade and found out myself. And I kept on searching till I found one that made logical sense. 

I learnt how many complex things worked, like an A/C, engines, toilets, pumps, etc. during high school by watching youtube explanations. One youtube channel called Lesics was especially good at this.

I enjoy reading interesting articles. Many, many hours spent doing so. There are some intelligent people out there with some great insights to share. You can check out a bunch of good ones I saved in my collection [here](https://github.com/kleenkanteen/interesting-articles).

## Being a debater
I enjoyed grade 12 english and I think I did well, my teacher gave me a solid review on my report card. She also made the class interesting so I was challenged. We had the choice to choose a book from a list of books to read, I chose Blink by Malcolm Gladwell, great book, very interesting stories of events and psychology experiments. I enjoyed the class. 

If you ask my friends, they'll say I will argue about something if I disagree. This requires boldness also. I'm detailed so I'll disect an argument and belief and question someone about how they got there. On youtube there's something I like watching called Street Epistemology. First search result on youtube of it will bring up the guy who does it.  I don't believe things blindly, I'll ask for sources. 

I only use reddit, no instagram at all, since reddit is a text heavy platform while instagram doens't even let you make text posts. On reddit I have long form convos with people and ask them a question and get a detailed written response back. Many time I've gotten interesting, multi paragraph long responses back. 

I try to be logically rigorous and provide sources for my claims. I'm passionate about finding the truth and having evidence based beliefs, so I've deveploped my debate and questioning skills to aid me in that pursuit.

For programming in particular, complexity is overwhelming when choosing what to use to build what idea. There's always tradeoffs to be made. Having those critical analysis skills has been valuable in this regard. I can know what tool to use for what job. And when a tool is overkill for a job. I've learn to start simple and minimize complexity.

My key layout, iterated and improved it around 15 times before settling on this one, I don't use QWERTY also, I switched right I got the keyboard July 2021. I use Colemak-DH as my fingers move about 50% of the distance due to the high frequency keys being on the home row. A graph over 5 months of my speed increase when I switched from QWERTY to Colemak-DH. I find it interesting I can see long term progress:

![colemak 5 month speed increase](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/67878058/184480416-aa502ad4-9bc6-4de4-800c-afa1444269f0.JPG)

There's several points of efficiency in this layout but to keep in short it has 2 layers. The first layer is at the top of the pic and the second is at the bottom of the pic. The second layer is activated with the MOD 1 key on the far right when it is held down like shift. Arrows keys under my left thumb are a treat for programming and typing in general. My arms don't gets displaced like a normal keyboard, only my fingers move, so it's very comfortable and ergonomic.
